01 · Foundations
Discovery
What is Elliott Wave Theory?
Developed by Ralph Nelson Elliott in the 1930s, Elliott Wave Theory proposes that financial markets move in recognizable, repetitive patterns driven by collective investor psychology. Elliott observed that price action unfolds in a series of waves — impulse waves that move in the direction of the main trend, and corrective waves that move against it.

The theory is grounded in the principle of fractal self-similarity: the same wave patterns appear across all timeframes, from minute charts to century-long market cycles. A complete market cycle consists of 8 waves: 5 impulse (motive) waves followed by 3 corrective waves.
Core Concept
The 5-3 Wave Structure
Every complete Elliott Wave cycle consists of two phases:

Motive Phase (5 waves) — Waves 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Waves 1, 3, and 5 move in the direction of the main trend (impulse waves). Waves 2 and 4 are corrections against the trend.

Corrective Phase (3 waves) — Waves A, B, C. Wave A moves against the prior trend, Wave B is a partial recovery, Wave C completes the correction and typically extends to match or exceed Wave A.
The complete cycle: 8 waves total — 5 up + 3 down (in a bull market)

02 · The Three Inviolable Rules
Rule Description Why it matters If violated
Rule 1

Wave 2 cannot retrace more than 100% of Wave 1		 Wave 2 must end above the starting point of Wave 1. If price falls below the origin of Wave 1, the count is invalid. Confirms the motive structure is still intact. A full retracement means Wave 1 was actually part of a larger correction. The wave count must be abandoned. Relabel — what you called Wave 1 was likely part of Wave A or C.
Rule 2

Wave 3 cannot be the shortest impulse wave		 Wave 3 must be longer than at least one of Wave 1 or Wave 5. Wave 3 is typically the most powerful and extended wave. Wave 3 is driven by broad participation and strong fundamentals — it reflects the core of the trend's momentum. If Wave 3 appears shortest, the count is wrong. Often means waves are mislabeled or you're in a corrective pattern.
Rule 3

Wave 4 cannot overlap Wave 1's price territory		 The low of Wave 4 must remain above the high of Wave 1. Any overlap invalidates the impulse structure (except in diagonal triangles). Ensures the motive wave maintains a clear directional structure. Overlap suggests a corrective or diagonal pattern instead. Re-examine — you may be in a leading diagonal, ending diagonal, or a completely different corrective structure.

Characteristics
Wave 1 is often the hardest to identify in real time. It emerges from the final stages of a prior trend and frequently appears as a simple counter-trend move. Volume may increase but not dramatically.

It subdivides into 5 smaller waves (impulse). Wave 1 is usually the shortest of waves 1, 3, and 5. Often retraced deeply by Wave 2 (50–61.8% Fibonacci).
Key Levels
Typical length: Shortest of the three impulse waves
Fibonacci target: 100% of prior move from bottom
Volume: Increasing but moderate · Sentiment: Still bearish/skeptical
Characteristics
Wave 2 is a corrective wave that retraces much of Wave 1. It can be a simple zigzag or a complex flat/combination. Sentiment often returns to near-bearish levels.

Crucially, Wave 2 cannot retrace 100% of Wave 1. It typically retraces 50–61.8%, creating a buying opportunity near support.
Key Levels
Hard floor: Start of Wave 1 (Rule 1)
Common retracement: 50% – 61.8% Fibonacci
Pattern: Zigzag (sharp) most common · Volume: Declining
Characteristics
Wave 3 is typically the strongest and longest wave. It represents the core of the trend — driven by fundamental confirmation, broad participation, and strong momentum. Breakouts occur here.

Often extends to 1.618× or 2.618× the length of Wave 1 (Fibonacci extension). Volume is highest during Wave 3. Cannot be the shortest impulse wave.
Key Levels
Minimum: Must exceed the end of Wave 1
Typical target: 1.618× Wave 1 from Wave 2 bottom
Volume: Highest of all waves · Sentiment: Increasingly bullish
Characteristics
Wave 4 is a sideways-to-down correction after the explosive Wave 3. It tends to be complex — often forming triangles, flats, or combinations. Alternation with Wave 2 is common.

Alternation guideline: If Wave 2 was a sharp zigzag, Wave 4 tends to be a flat or triangle — and vice versa. This is not a rule, but holds in most cases.
Key Levels
Hard floor: High of Wave 1 (Rule 3 — no overlap)
Common retracement: 38.2% of Wave 3
Pattern: Often complex (triangle, flat) · Alternation with Wave 2
Characteristics
Wave 5 is the final impulse wave. It typically shows weakening momentum despite making new highs. Divergence on RSI and MACD is common. Sentiment is often at peak optimism (or pessimism in a bear market).

Wave 5 often equals Wave 1 in length, or targets the 0.618× extension of Waves 1+3. After Wave 5 completes, a full ABC correction begins.
Key Levels
Common length: Equal to Wave 1, or 0.618× (W1+W3)
Warning: RSI divergence = Wave 5 exhaustion signal
Volume: Often lower than Wave 3 · Sentiment: Extreme optimism
Characteristics
Wave A is the first wave of the corrective sequence. In a bull market, it is the initial decline after the 5-wave advance. Many participants interpret it as a buying dip.

Wave A subdivides into 5 waves (in a zigzag) or 3 waves (in a flat). The subdivision helps identify which corrective pattern is forming.
Pattern Identification
5-sub-waves → Likely a Zigzag forming
3-sub-waves → Likely a Flat or Triangle forming
Sentiment: Disbelief — "just a pullback" narrative dominates
Characteristics
Wave B is a counter-trend recovery within the corrective phase. It retraces part of Wave A, tempting traders into thinking the uptrend has resumed. Wave B is often the most deceptive wave.

In a flat, Wave B may retrace 90–100%+ of Wave A (expanded flat). In a zigzag, it typically retraces 38–61.8%. Never goes beyond the start of Wave A in a zigzag.
Key Levels
Zigzag: B retraces 38.2%–61.8% of A
Flat: B retraces 81%–100%+ of A (expanded)
Volume: Low — a weak recovery. Sentiment: False optimism returns
Characteristics
Wave C is the final and most destructive corrective wave. It moves in the same direction as Wave A and typically equals Wave A in length, or extends to 1.618× Wave A. Always subdivides into 5 waves.

Wave C completes the full ABC corrective structure and sets up the next larger-degree impulse wave. This is the prime entry zone for position building.
Key Levels
Target: Equal to Wave A, or 1.618× Wave A
Always 5 sub-waves — confirms corrective end
Sentiment: Maximum pessimism — best buying opportunity in the cycle
04 · Corrective Patterns
Pattern A
Zigzag (5-3-5)
The sharpest corrective pattern. Wave A subdivides into 5 waves, Wave B retraces only 38–61.8% of A, and Wave C subdivides into 5 waves, typically equaling Wave A in length.

Most common in Wave 2 corrections. Zigzags create deep, fast retracements that look like strong counter-trend moves.
Structure: 5-3-5 · B retraces <61.8% of A · C ≈ A or 1.618A
Pattern B
Flat (3-3-5)
A sideways correction. Wave A subdivides into 3 waves, Wave B retraces 81–100%+ of Wave A, and Wave C subdivides into 5 waves, ending near (regular flat) or beyond (expanded flat) the start of Wave A.

Most common in Wave 4 corrections. Flats are less steep and often misidentified as continuation patterns.
Structure: 3-3-5 · B retraces 81–100%+ of A · Expanded: C exceeds A start
Pattern C
Triangle (3-3-3-3-3)
A contracting or expanding sideways pattern with 5 sub-waves (A-B-C-D-E), each subdividing into 3 waves. Price oscillates between converging (or diverging) trendlines.

Typically appears in Wave 4 or Wave B. After the triangle completes, the breakout wave (Wave 5 or Wave C) tends to be swift and equal to the widest part of the triangle.
After triangle: Thrust = widest part of the triangle · Fast move
Pattern D
Double & Triple Zigzag
Complex corrections linking two or three zigzags with connecting waves (labeled X). These are the most difficult patterns to identify in real time and can last much longer than simple corrections.

Common when markets need to correct a powerful Wave 3 move. Often seen in Wave 4 or Wave 2 of higher-degree wave counts.
W-X-Y (double) or W-X-Y-X-Z (triple) · Time-consuming corrections
